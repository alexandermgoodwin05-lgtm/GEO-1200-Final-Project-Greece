Homepage / Introduction
Greece is a Mediterranean country located at the crossroads of southeastern Europe, the Aegean Sea, the Ionian Sea, 
and the eastern Mediterranean. According to the European Union country profile, Greece covers 131,694 square 
kilometers and has a population of about 10.41 million people. It has been an EU member since 1981, uses the euro, 
and is a parliamentary republic. Greece also has a lower GDP per capita than the EU average, which matters because 
disaster preparedness depends not only on scientific knowledge, but also on the money and administrative capacity 
needed to strengthen buildings, protect forests, improve warning systems, and recover after disasters (European 
Union, 2026).
The central argument of this website is that Greece faces two overlapping categories of natural hazards. First, Greece 
is one of the most seismically active parts of Europe because the Aegean region is being deformed by plate 
interactions between Africa, Eurasia, and the Anatolian/Aegean region. This creates earthquake, tsunami, and volcanic 
hazards. Second, Greece has a Mediterranean climate with hot, dry summers and intense rainfall events, which creates 
major wildfire, heat, drought, and flood risks. Greece is a developed EU country with strong scientific institutions and 
access to European assistance, but its long coastline, thousands of islands, older infrastructure, tourism dependence, 
and economic constraints make disaster management difficult.

Section 1 - Why Greece Is Vulnerable
Greece is vulnerable because its physical geography concentrates people, infrastructure, and economic activity in 
hazardous places. Greece has more than 6,000 islands, 227 of which are inhabited; about 15% of the national 
population lives on islands. Islands create special disaster-response problems because evacuation, firefighting, medical 
access, electrical interconnection, water supply, and post-disaster recovery may depend on ports, ferries, airports, and 
weather conditions (European Commission Clean Energy for EU Islands, 2022).
The coastline and islands also increase tsunami and sea-level-rise exposure. UNESCO-IOC reports that Greece has more 
than 8.8 million people in coastal areas, approximately 85% of the total population. That means even rare coastal 
hazards can affect a large share of the country (UNESCO-IOC, 2026).
Greece also has a strong economic exposure to coastal tourism. Many hotels, ports, roads, historic sites, and visitor 
areas are located near the coast or on islands. When a hazard disrupts ferry service, airports, water supply, power, or 
coastal roads, the effects are not only physical; they also affect employment, tourism revenue, and local government 
capacity to respond.
Finally, Greece is exposed to both sudden-onset and slow-onset hazards. Earthquakes and tsunamis can happen with 
little warning. Wildfires and floods may be forecast hours to days in advance, but fast wind shifts or extreme rainfall 
can overwhelm local plans. Drought, sea-level rise, and heat waves develop more slowly, but they increase wildfire 
risk, water scarcity, agricultural stress, and public-health risk over time.

Section 2 - Earthquakes: Greece’s Highest Priority Geologic Hazard
Earthquakes are the most important geologic hazard for Greece because seismic activity is frequent and because rare 
large events can cause severe casualties and economic loss. The World Bank/GFDRR disaster risk profile for Greece 
states that floods occur more regularly and create recurring economic losses, but earthquakes represent the most 
severe risk because they can produce large-scale damage and casualties during rare but powerful events (GFDRR & 
World Bank, 2017).
Greece’s earthquake hazard comes from the complicated tectonics of the eastern Mediterranean. The African Plate is 
moving northward beneath the Aegean region along the Hellenic subduction zone, while extension within the Aegean 
and interactions with the Anatolian region produce many active faults. The result is a mix of subduction-related 
deformation, normal faulting, strike-slip faulting, crustal extension, uplift, subsidence, and offshore earthquakes. This is 
why damaging earthquakes can occur both on land and under the sea.
A major recent example is the October 30, 2020 Samos earthquake. The USGS records it as a magnitude 7.0 
earthquake located offshore north-northeast of Neon Karlovasion, Greece, at a depth of about 21 km. The USGS 
tectonic summary states that the earthquake occurred as normal faulting at shallow crustal depth within the Eurasia 
tectonic plate in the eastern Aegean Sea. This earthquake caused serious damage in both Greece and Turkey and also 
showed that offshore earthquakes can trigger secondary hazards such as liquefaction, landslides, coastal deformation, 
and tsunami concerns (USGS, 2020).
Research after the Samos earthquake documented primary and secondary environmental effects on Samos Island, 
including coseismic uplift on rocky coasts and port facilities, surface ruptures, slope failures, liquefaction, ground 
cracks, and hydrological anomalies. These effects show that earthquake impacts are not limited to building shaking; 
they can also damage harbors, roads, slopes, shorelines, and water systems (Mavroulis et al., 2021).
Greece has a strong earthquake monitoring system. The National Observatory of Athens states that its Seismicity 
Monitoring Unit continuously monitors seismicity in the wider Greek area 24 hours a day, 365 days a year, and 
disseminates information to state bodies and the public. The Hellenic Unified Seismic Network provides near-real-time 
seismological data from more than 150 stations (National Observatory of Athens, 2026a; 2026b).
Analysis: Greece cannot predict the exact time and location of earthquakes, but it can reduce earthquake losses 
through monitoring, hazard maps, building codes, retrofitting, emergency drills, and public education. The largest 
weakness is not scientific knowledge; it is implementation. Older buildings, historic structures, narrow streets, dense 
urban areas, and remote islands are difficult to upgrade. Earthquakes should remain the top geologic priority because 
they can trigger other hazards and because a major event near Athens, Thessaloniki, Crete, or a major island would 
create national-scale consequences.

Section 3 - Tsunamis
Tsunamis are less frequent than earthquakes or wildfires, but they matter greatly in Greece because so much of the 
population and economy is coastal. UNESCO-IOC reports that approximately 85% of Greece’s population is coastal, and 
Greece participates in the Mediterranean and northeastern Atlantic tsunami warning system. The National 
Observatory of Athens acts as a Tsunami Service Provider for the broader NEAM region and also hosts Greece’s 
National Tsunami Warning Center. Samos is listed by UNESCO-IOC as a Greek community recognized as Tsunami Ready 
(UNESCO-IOC, 2026).
Greece’s tsunami hazard is mainly connected to offshore earthquakes, submarine landslides, and volcanic processes. 
The most important historical case is the 1956 Amorgos earthquake and tsunami in the south-central Aegean Sea. A 
peer-reviewed study in Geophysical Journal International describes the July 9, 1956 Amorgos event as the largest 
earthquake to strike Greece in the twentieth century, with a magnitude around 7.8, 53 deaths, heavy damage, and a 
tsunami that affected the Cyclades, Dodecanese, Crete, and the Turkish coast. The study reports run-up values of 
about 30 m on the southern coast of Amorgos, 20 m on Astypalaia, and 10 m on Folegandros (Okal et al., 2009).
More recent research published in Communications Earth & Environment identified a large seafloor rupture associated 
with the 1956 Amorgos tsunamigenic earthquake. The study found a 75-km-long Amorgos fault rupture and 
emphasized that the Mediterranean has a high probability of a large earthquake-triggered tsunami in coming decades. 
This reinforces that tsunami risk in Greece is scientifically credible, not just historical (Leclerc et al., 2024).
Analysis: Greece has the scientific and institutional basis for tsunami warning, but the practical challenge is warning 
time. In the Aegean, a tsunami generated close to an island may arrive quickly, leaving little time for evacuation. This 
makes public education, signage, evacuation routes, and automatic alerts essential. The warning system matters, but it 
cannot replace local preparedness.
Section 4 - Volcanic Hazards
Greece has volcanic hazards because the Hellenic Volcanic Arc crosses the southern Aegean. The Smithsonian Global 
Volcanism Program lists five Holocene volcanoes in Greece: Santorini, Kolumbo, Methana, Milos, and Nisyros. These 
volcanoes are part of the Hellenic Volcanic Arc and show that Greece has active or potentially active volcanic systems, 
even though eruptions are infrequent compared with earthquakes and wildfires (Smithsonian Institution, 2026a).
Santorini is the most important volcanic hazard for the public because of its explosive eruption history, caldera setting, 
dense tourism, and location in the Aegean. The Smithsonian profile lists Santorini in Greece’s Hellenic Volcanic Arc, 
with a last known eruption in 1950 CE. Santorini’s caldera walls expose deposits from multiple caldera-forming 
eruptions, including the famous Bronze Age/Minoan eruption around 3,500 years ago (Smithsonian Institution, 2026b).
The main volcanic hazards are ashfall, volcanic gases, earthquakes related to volcanic unrest, ground deformation, 
landslides, submarine volcanic activity, and tsunami generation. Even a moderate eruption could seriously disrupt air 
travel, ferry traffic, tourism, water supply, and evacuation logistics. On Santorini, the problem is not only eruption size; 
it is exposure. Millions of visitors pass through the island in many years, and the island’s steep caldera topography and 
limited routes create evacuation complexity.
Nisyros and other volcanic centers also matter because they are populated or near populated islands. Nisyros has 
hydrothermal activity and a history of phreatic eruptions. Even without a large magmatic eruption, gas release, 
hydrothermal explosions, and earthquakes could threaten local residents and tourists.
Analysis: Volcanic hazards are lower probability than earthquakes and wildfires, but consequences could be very high 
in the Santorini area. Monitoring should focus on seismic swarms, ground deformation, gas emissions, and 
hydrothermal activity. Greece’s challenge is communicating uncertainty: volcanic unrest may not lead to eruption, but 
officials must still plan for evacuation, tourist management, and port/airport disruption.
Section 5 - Wildfires
Wildfires are one of Greece’s most urgent present-day hazards. Greece’s Mediterranean climate creates hot, dry 
summers, and climate change is increasing the danger of extreme heat, dry vegetation, and longer fire seasons. Strong 
winds can turn a small ignition into a fast-moving disaster, especially near communities, forests, and tourist areas.
The 2023 wildfire season shows why this hazard belongs near the top of Greece’s risk ranking. Copernicus reports that 
Europe had above-average fire danger for much of 2023 and that Greece experienced the largest wildfire ever 
recorded in Europe, around 96,000 hectares, near Alexandroupolis. Copernicus also states that the 2023 EU wildfire 
season had about 500,000 hectares burned, the fourth largest EU burned area on record (Copernicus Climate Change 
Service, 2024a).
The European Commission’s Civil Protection and Humanitarian Aid department reported that the Alexandroupolis 
wildfire was the largest in the EU since EFFIS records began in 2000. Greece activated the EU Civil Protection 
Mechanism, and the EU mobilized 11 rescEU firefighting planes, one helicopter, and ground firefighting teams from 
European countries (European Commission, 2023).
Copernicus Sentinel-2 imagery also documented the fire near Alexandroupolis and Dadia National Park. Its report 
stated that the fire had claimed 21 lives and forced the evacuation of 15,000 people by late August 2023. This shows 
how wildfire risk is not only environmental: it directly affects human life, tourism, public health, biodiversity, 
transportation, and international assistance needs (Copernicus, 2023).
Analysis: Wildfire is probably Greece’s highest climate-related disaster risk because it is recurring, fast-moving, and 
becoming more severe under hot and dry conditions. Greece can forecast fire danger better than it can forecast exact 
fire behavior, but the response challenge is huge when many fires ignite at once. The biggest mitigation priorities are 
fuel management, land-use planning, public enforcement during high-risk weather, defensible space around 
communities, improved evacuation routes, and investment in firefighting capacity before the summer season.
Section 6 - Flooding
Flooding is a frequent and economically important hazard in Greece. The World Bank/GFDRR profile notes that floods 
occur more regularly than major earthquakes and lead to recurring economic losses. Flooding can occur when intense 
rainfall falls over steep terrain, narrow valleys, urban surfaces, or agricultural plains with limited drainage. Flash floods 
can also occur in burned landscapes because vegetation loss and soil changes increase runoff.
Storm Daniel in September 2023 is the strongest recent example. Copernicus reports that after a drier-than-average 
summer with heatwaves and wildfires, Greece, Bulgaria, and Turkey were hit by record-breaking rainfall and flooding. 
From September 4-6, parts of Greece received record rainfall; in Zagora, Thessaly, 754 mm fell in 21 hours, including 
528 mm in 10 hours. Copernicus describes this as the highest daily rainfall ever recorded in Greece, with some stations 
recording more than 800 mm over three days and maxima of 1,024 mm and 1,156 mm. Satellite estimates indicated 
about 700 square kilometers flooded, at least 17 deaths in Greece, around 5,000 displaced people, and major losses to 
crops, livestock, infrastructure, homes, and businesses (Copernicus Climate Change Service, 2024b).
Analysis: Flooding exposes a weakness in Greece’s adaptation system. Unlike earthquakes, where hazard awareness is 
long-standing, flood risk is changing as climate extremes intensify and as urbanization and land-use changes place 
more assets in flood-prone areas. Greece needs stronger floodplain planning, updated flood maps, retention basins, 
urban drainage upgrades, river restoration, and better post-wildfire runoff management. Storm Daniel also shows that 
planning based only on past averages may underestimate future extreme rainfall.
Section 7 - Heat Waves, Drought, Water Scarcity, and Sea-Level Rise
Heat waves and drought are slow-building hazards that make other disasters worse. Extreme heat increases wildfire 
danger, stresses older adults and outdoor workers, raises electricity demand, worsens air pollution, and harms tourism 
during peak summer. Drought reduces soil moisture and vegetation health, increases irrigation pressure, and worsens 
water scarcity on islands and in agricultural regions.
The European Commission explains that water scarcity and droughts are becoming more frequent and widespread in 
the EU and that climate change is expected to further deteriorate water resources as temperatures rise. Water scarcity 
occurs when demand frequently exceeds sustainable natural supply, and drought impacts become worse where water 
resources are already low or poorly managed (European Commission, 2026).
The EEA/Climate-ADAPT Greece health profile shows that Greece already treats heat as a public-health adaptation 
issue. Greece’s Ministry of Health issues public-health instructions for extreme weather such as floods, forest fires, 
severe heat, and heat waves; during severe heat, cooler spaces may be made available to the public. The profile also 
notes concerns about disease risks such as West Nile virus as temperatures rise (European Environment Agency 
Climate-ADAPT, 2026).
Sea-level rise is a slower hazard, but it directly affects Greece because of the country’s long coast, ports, beaches, 
coastal tourism, and island communities. Sea-level rise can worsen erosion, saltwater intrusion, coastal flooding, and 
storm-surge damage. Even without a single catastrophic event, gradual coastal change can damage beaches, 
archaeological sites, roads, harbors, wetlands, and tourism infrastructure.
Analysis: Heat and drought should be treated as threat multipliers. They make wildfires more likely, increase water
supply stress, reduce agricultural productivity, and make disaster recovery harder. Greece’s adaptation response 
should include heat-health warning systems, urban shade, cool public spaces, water conservation, leak reduction, 
drought planning, agricultural adaptation, and protection of coastal zones from erosion and sea-level rise.
Section 8 - Greece's Disaster Response Capabilities
Greece has several important strengths. It is an EU member state, which gives it access to the EU Civil Protection 
Mechanism and rescEU assets during major disasters. This was visible during the 2023 wildfires, when Greece received 
European aircraft and ground teams. Greece also has strong scientific institutions, including the National Observatory 
of Athens, the Institute of Geodynamics, university seismic networks, and the Hellenic National Tsunami Warning 
Centre.
Greece also uses the European 112 emergency number. The Ministry for Climate Crisis and Civil Protection explains 
that 112 is an integrated communication service for emergency assistance and can send mass alerts to citizens when a 
natural disaster or dangerous situation is expected or underway. Alerts can include protection guidelines and 
instructions. Greece’s 112 outbound system can use cell broadcast, SMS, e-mail, and voice alerts (Ministry for Climate 
Crisis and Civil Protection, 2026a; 2026b).
Greece’s monitoring capacity is strongest for earthquakes, tsunami alerts, weather warnings, and fire-danger mapping. 
Near-real-time seismic monitoring helps officials rapidly locate earthquakes and assess shaking. Copernicus and EFFIS 
provide satellite-based wildfire and flood information. The 112 system can help authorities move warnings quickly to 
residents and visitors.
However, response capability is limited by geography and event complexity. Thousands of islands create 
transportation and evacuation issues. A wildfire on Rhodes, a tsunami warning for Samos, and a flood in Thessaly all 
require different response systems. During compound disasters, such as heat plus wildfire plus smoke plus tourist 
evacuation, even a developed country can be overwhelmed.
Analysis: Greece’s disaster capability is relatively strong compared with many countries, but the key challenge is scaling 
response across islands, rural regions, and tourist zones. The most useful improvement is not just more emergency 
response; it is reducing disaster demand before events happen through building retrofits, land management, flood 
defenses, public education, and reliable local evacuation planning.
Section 9 - Social, Political, and Economic Factors
Social factors: Greece has a large coastal population and many tourists who may not understand local hazards, 
language, evacuation routes, or the 112 alert system. Older adults, people with medical needs, low-income residents, 
seasonal workers, and tourists without cars are especially vulnerable during heat waves, wildfires, floods, and 
evacuations. Public education must be multilingual and designed for residents and visitors.
Political factors: Greece is an EU member and parliamentary republic, which supports coordination with European 
agencies and funding sources. EU membership is a major advantage during large events because Greece can request 
international assistance through EU civil protection systems. However, EU membership also creates obligations. Flood 
planning, climate adaptation, building enforcement, and environmental management require continuous 
administrative follow-through, not just emergency response after disasters.
Economic factors: Greece’s GDP per capita is below the EU average. This matters because resilience projects are 
expensive: retrofitting old buildings, maintaining forests, modernizing water systems, improving drainage, and building 
coastal defenses all require long-term funding. The economic shock of disasters can also hurt tourism, agriculture, 
insurance markets, and public budgets.
Geographic inequality: Island and remote communities may have fewer resources than Athens or other major 
mainland cities. Small islands can be difficult to evacuate and may rely on limited ports, ferry schedules, and seasonal 
staffing. Disaster planning must therefore be local, not only national.
Conclusion for this section: Greece’s vulnerability is not caused by lack of knowledge. Greece has strong scientists, 
monitoring networks, and EU support. The vulnerability comes from exposure, implementation cost, island geography, 
aging infrastructure, tourism pressure, and the increasing intensity of climate-related extremes.
Section 10 - Recommendations
1. Prioritize earthquake retrofitting and code enforcement
Greece should focus on schools, hospitals, ports, emergency facilities, older apartment blocks, and historic structures. 
Monitoring helps after earthquakes, but the biggest life-safety gains come from stronger buildings before shaking 
begins.
2. Expand wildfire prevention, not only suppression
More aircraft help during fires, but prevention reduces the number of catastrophic events. Priorities include fuel 
breaks, vegetation management near communities, stricter ignition controls during red-flag conditions, defensible 
space, and forest restoration after fires.
3. Improve flood adaptation after Storm Daniel
Greece should update flood maps, improve urban drainage, restore floodplains where possible, protect agricultural 
plains, and strengthen rainfall/runoff modeling. Flood planning should account for extreme rainfall outside historical 
expectations.
4. Strengthen island evacuation and communication plans
Every high-risk island should have clear tsunami, wildfire, earthquake, and evacuation maps. Plans should be 
multilingual and visible in hotels, ferry ports, airports, beaches, and rental properties.
5. Integrate tourism into disaster planning
Tourists increase exposure but may not know local risks. Hotels and tour operators should be required to explain 112 
alerts, evacuation routes, heat-safety guidance, and wildfire restrictions.
6. Treat heat and drought as public-health hazards
Heat action plans should include shaded cooling centers, outreach to older adults, worker protections, water 
conservation, public drinking-water points, and urban greening.
7. Use EU and Copernicus data more aggressively at the local level
Satellite wildfire, flood, heat, and drought information should be turned into local action dashboards that 
municipalities can understand and use quickly.
Conclusion
Greece is a strong final-project country because it faces multiple major hazards that connect directly to course topics: 
earthquakes, tsunamis, volcanoes, wildfires, floods, heat waves, drought, and sea-level rise. The two greatest overall 
priorities are earthquakes and wildfires. Earthquakes are the most severe geologic risk because Greece’s tectonic 
setting can produce destructive shaking and secondary hazards. Wildfires are the most urgent recurring climate risk 
because they are already causing evacuations, fatalities, ecological damage, tourism disruption, and EU-level 
emergency response needs.
Flooding should also be treated as a high priority after Storm Daniel because it revealed how extreme rainfall can 
overwhelm communities, agriculture, and infrastructure. Tsunamis and volcanoes are lower-frequency hazards, but 
their consequences could be severe in the Aegean because of coastal populations, islands, ports, tourism, and limited 
warning time. Heat, drought, water scarcity, and sea-level rise are slower hazards, but they amplify nearly every other 
risk.
The most important conclusion is that Greece’s disaster risk is not only a natural-science issue. It is also social, political, 
and economic. Greece has strong monitoring systems and EU support, but resilience depends on whether the country 
can afford, enforce, and maintain long-term mitigation measures across mainland cities, rural areas, and hundreds of 
inhabited islands.
